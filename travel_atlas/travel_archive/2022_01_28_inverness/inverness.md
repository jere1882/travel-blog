## Inverness: The gateway to the Highlands

After my first trip to Edinburgh in 2021, it quickly became clear that I wanted to explore more of Scotland‚Äîand the opportunity arrived sooner than expected: a 10-day window to work remotely during the British winter.

I first heard of Inverness through the TV series _Outlander_, and a little research revealed its reputation as "the gateway to the Scottish Highlands." Perfectly positioned for exploring iconic Scottish landmarks‚ÄîLoch Ness, Skye Island, Urquhart Castle‚Äîthe city also offers its own charm, with scenic views stretching across the River Ness.

I booked two inexpensive EasyJet flights from Gatwick (around ¬£40 each way), secured a cozy Airbnb, and braved the bitter Scottish winter. What I discovered was more than just a picturesque city: over time, Inverness has become a sanctuary for me, a place to reflect, make big decisions, and simply find calm amid the beauty of the Highlands.

### Inverness

Inverness is a rather small city, with a population of 45000 and a compact, scenic city center. The city is split in two by the River Ness, and I was lucky enough to get a great apartment one block away from the Old Town, right across the river.

![[img_inverness_2022_004.jpeg]]

Bridge Street is my favourite street in the city centre, you can find branches of most British franchises in the atmospheric cobbled streets. My absolute favourite coffee shop is Cafe Nero, where I would get a spot next to the window each morning and see the snow fall.

![[img_inverness_2022_022.jpeg]]

![[img_inverness_2022_002.jpeg]]

### Day Trips: Loch Ness, Fort Augustus, Castles and Skye Island

I reserved a spot in one of Rabbie's day trips to visit the famous Loch Ness, where the monster Nessie is supposed to live. To be honest, the lake is nondescript and the landmark itself is a tourist trap. Of course, you're not going to see anything resembling Nessie, other than a lot of inventive souvenirs.
![[img_inverness_2022_016.jpeg]]

Excluding Loch Ness, the tour was really good: great views of the Scottish countryside, which looks magical and eerie in winter due to the mist and shifting weather.

The gem of this day trip was **Urquhart Castle**, one of the most iconic castles about 20 km south west of Inverness.

Founded around the 13th Century, it overlooks Loch Ness and offers stunning views. It is nowadays a medieval ruin, featuring walls, towers and gatehouses.

![[img_inverness_2022_024.jpeg]]

One day I hope to visit the ruins on my own and take my time to stroll around. The guided tour only allowed a brief stop.

On a second day trip, I got to visit a slightly less-ruined casle: **Eilean Donan castle**. It's imposingly located on a small island and connected to the mainland by a stone bridge.

![[img_inverness_2022_025.jpeg]]

Built in the 13th century to defend against Viking invasions, it was destroyed in 1719 during the Jacobite uprising and subsequently restored in the early 20th Century.

## Feelings

I landed in Inverness at a turning point in my life. I was caught in an existential crisis, weighed down by anxiety and a heavy sadness I‚Äôd never known before. Still, I remember walking along the frozen path by the river after leaving the gym, and for a moment, finding peace in the quiet stillness of the city.

Even in that dark time, I managed to see the beauty of the Highlands, and that meant something. Catching a glimpse of beauty‚Äîeven if only briefly‚Äîfelt like a small lifeline.

I returned to Inverness years later, not just for the landscape but to rewrite the difficult memories of that first visit. I stayed in the same apartment, walked the same streets, and gave the past a different ending‚Äîone filled with hope instead of despair. Three years on, I was more mature, more prepared to face whatever life would bring. Pain, I realized, is rarely wasted; it often carves the space we need to grow.
